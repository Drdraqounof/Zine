Before We Begin

Know Your
Rights

What is Equal Pay?
Equal pay means the same compensation for the same job, regardless of gender, race, or identity.
  • The Equal Pay Act of 1963 prohibits paying women less than men for equal work at the same company.
  • Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 also prohibits wage discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.
  • Many states have pay transparency laws and salary history bans that go beyond federal protections.
💡 Key Law

The Equal Pay Act requires equal pay for "substantially equal" work. You don't need the same job title — just similar responsibilities and skill requirements.

What Are Women's Rights at Work?
  • You cannot be fired, demoted, or passed over because of your gender.
  • Pregnancy discrimination is illegal under the Pregnancy Discrimination Act.
  • Sexual harassment is a form of sex discrimination and is illegal under Title VII.
  • Retaliation for reporting discrimination is itself illegal.
Race Discrimination Rights
  • Title VII prohibits discrimination based on race, color, or national origin in hiring, firing, promotions, and pay.
  • File complaints with the EEOC — the federal agency that enforces these laws.
  • Hostile work environments based on race are illegal — slurs, derogatory comments, and race-based harassment all qualify.
Maya's Story · Chapter 1
The Number
She Wasn't
Supposed to See
Scene · Conference Room B · Tuesday 10am
Maya had been a software developer at Nexum Technologies for three years. Two months ago she'd quietly led a cloud migration that saved the company $400,000 annually.
The shared drive folder was mislabeled. Instead of meeting notes, she found a salary spreadsheet — all five developers on her team with their compensation.
What Maya Discovered
$25,000
Less per year than her male colleagues with the same title and start date.
Four names above hers. Same title: Senior Software Developer. All white men. She is a Black woman. The gap sat at the intersection of race and sex discrimination.
Maya, to herself "Same job, same title, same company. So why is this number so different?"
Maya's Story · Chapter 2
She Didn't
React. She
Prepared.
Scene · Maya's Home · That Evening
Maya knew better than to storm into HR with nothing but anger. She built her case methodically, with documentation.
📁 Her Documentation
Screenshot of the salary spreadsheet (saved off company devices) · Three years of "Exceeds Expectations" reviews · Emails crediting her cloud migration · Original offer letter · Written promotion timeline
She also discovered her city had a Pay Transparency Ordinance — employees had the right to discuss wages, and Nexum was required to post salary ranges. They had not been doing this.
⚖️ Laws That Applied
Federal: Equal Pay Act (1963), Title VII (1964)
City: Pay Transparency Ordinance, Ban on Salary History
State: Equal Pay Act covering race and ethnicity
Maya "I'm asking for what the law says I'm owed. And I'm walking in there ready to prove it."
Maya's Story · Chapter 3
The HR
Meeting She
Ran Herself
Scene · HR Office · Thursday 2pm
Maya came with a printed folder — two copies of everything. She was calm, specific, and did not apologize for being there.
Maya "Colleagues in the same role are earning significantly more. I'd like the basis for that difference in writing."
The HR manager suggested "negotiation differences." Maya cited the Equal Pay Act: the employer must show the gap is based on seniority, merit, or a factor other than sex.
🛡️ HR Works for the Company
Always document everything. Follow up every conversation with an email summary. Never sign anything without reviewing it first.
That afternoon she sent a follow-up email summarizing the discussion, her documentation, and a formal request for a written response. She also filed a preliminary inquiry with the city labor office and the EEOC — a protective measure that timestamps her complaint.
Maya's Story · Chapter 4
What
Happened
Next
Three Weeks Later
The city's Pay Transparency audit, triggered by Maya's filing, found Nexum had failed to post salary ranges — a separate violation increasing pressure to settle.
Maya's Resolution
Back Pay + Raise
Salary adjustment to parity, two years of retroactive back pay, and a company-wide policy review — all before any lawsuit was filed.
Maya shared her experience with two other women of color on her team. Within six months, three more pay equity investigations were opened at Nexum.
Maya "The law was already on my side — I just had to learn it well enough to use it."
📋 Your Steps If This Happens to You
1. Document everything off company devices.
2. Research your local laws — city and state often go further than federal.
3. Request an HR meeting in writing; follow up with an email summary.
4. File with the EEOC (eeoc.gov) and your city labor office.
5. Consult an employment attorney — many offer free consultations.
6. Don't sign anything without reviewing it with an attorney first.

What Happens When Leadership Gets Involved
Maya's case didn't stop at her raise. Because her manager and leadership escalated internally and took accountability, systemic change started.
🔍 Level 1: Recognition
Manager sees: Pay gaps in their team data. Turnover patterns among women and people of color. Compensation doesn't align with performance reviews.
📊 Level 2: Data Audit
Director pulls: Full compensation analysis by race, gender, tenure. Compare pay to market rates. Identify where disparities cluster.
⚡ Level 3: Executive Accountability
C-suite takes action: Hire independent pay equity consultant. Create remediation plan. Board gets briefed on legal exposure. Budget allocated for back pay and raises.
At Nexum: What Changed
6 months
From Maya's complaint to company-wide salary adjustment affecting 23 employees. Policy: annual pay equity audits, salary bands posted, salary history bans.
Individual courage becomes organizational accountability becomes systemic change.
💼 For Leaders: How to Be the One Who Escalates
If you're a manager: Run your own pay equity check. If gaps exist, escalate to HR and your director with data, not defensiveness.

If you're in HR or leadership: Don't wait for complaints. Audit compensation proactively. When you find disparities, escalate with urgency — legal liability grows with delay.

If you're at the executive level: Make pay equity a board-level priority. Set budget for remediation. Require transparency. Show that your company is serious about equity, not just compliance.

Allyship at every level: Speak up about patterns you see. Support the people filing complaints. Don't let HR bury it. Push back on delays. Connected escalation is what turns one person's fight into systemic change.
The Principle "Individual worker courage opens the door. Leadership accountability walks through it. That's systemic change."
